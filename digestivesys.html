1️⃣ Oral Cavity (Ingestion) 👄

📖 Key Definitions

  • Bolus: Soft mass of chewed food mixed with saliva, ready for swallowing
  • Mastication: Mechanical breakdown of food by teeth and jaw muscles
  • Deglutition: The act of swallowing; moves food from mouth to stomach
  • Salivary Amylase (Ptyalin): Enzyme beginning carbohydrate digestion (starch → maltose)
  • Lingual Lipase: Enzyme activating minor fat digestion in the mouth

Anatomy Overview

Structure Description Function
Teeth 32 permanent (incisors, canines, premolars, molars) Mechanical breakdown
Tongue Muscular organ with papillae/taste buds Manipulation, bolus formation
Palate Hard (bone) and soft (muscle) Separates oral/nasal cavities
Parotid Gland Largest, serous secretion Watery saliva (enzyme-rich)
Submandibular Mixed serous/mucous Moderate volume saliva
Sublingual Smallest, mucous secretion Thick, lubricating saliva
Salivation Stats:
• Production: 1-1.5 L/day
• Stimulated by: Sight, smell, taste, chewing
• pH: 6.2-7.4 (neutral to slightly acidic)

Radiographic Relevance

Structure Examination Indications
Mouth/Teeth Panoramic X-rays, occlusal views Mandible fractures, dental pathology
Salivary Glands Sialography Ductal system evaluation, stones
Pharynx Barium swallow (pharyngography) Dysphagia, aspiration risk

2️⃣ Pharynx & Esophagus (Propulsion) 🛤️

📖 Key Definitions

  • Upper Esophageal Sphincter (UES): Cricopharyngeus muscle at C6; prevents air entry
  • Lower Esophageal Sphincter (LES): Physiological sphincter at T10-T11; prevents reflux
  • Peristalsis: Wave-like muscular contractions propelling food downward
  • Primary Peristalsis: Bolus-induced; initiated by swallowing
  • Secondary Peristalsis: Esophageal distension-induced; clears residual material
  • Tertiary Contractions: Non-propulsive contractions (pathological if excessive)

Esophageal Anatomy

Feature Description
Length 25 cm (10 inches)
Upper 1/3 Striated muscle (voluntary control)
Middle 1/3 Mixed striated/smooth muscle
Lower 1/3 Smooth muscle (involuntary)

Esophageal Constrictions (Narrowing Points)

Level Location Vertebral Level Clinical Note
1st (Narrowest) Cricoid cartilage C6 Common site for foreign body impaction
2nd Aortic arch/Left main bronchus T4-T5 Impression visible on barium swallow
3rd Diaphragmatic hiatus T10 Hiatal hernia location
💡 Memory Tip: "6-4-10 Rule" - Constrictions at C6, T4-T5, T10. Think: "Cricoid, Aortic, Diaphragm"

Swallowing Phases (Deglutition)

Phase Type Duration Key Events
Oral Voluntary Variable Tongue propels bolus to oropharynx
Pharyngeal Involuntary ~1 second Soft palate elevation, laryngeal rise, epiglottis closure, UES relaxation
Esophageal Involuntary 6-10 seconds Primary peristalsis → LES relaxation → gastric entry

Radiographic Examinations

Exam Technique Pathology Detected
Barium Swallow Single/double contrast Strictures, masses, hiatal hernia
Video Fluoroscopy Dynamic swallowing study Aspiration, motility disorders
Esophagram Multiple positions (upright, prone) Varices, achalasia, reflux

3️⃣ Stomach (Storage & Chemical Digestion) 🫃

📖 Key Definitions

  • Chyme: Semi-liquid acidic mixture (pH 2) of partially digested food leaving stomach
  • Rugae: Longitudinal mucosal folds that flatten with stomach distension
  • Pyloric Sphincter: Muscular valve controlling chyme release into duodenum
  • Receptive Relaxation: Vagal reflex allowing stomach to accommodate food without pressure increase
  • Intrinsic Factor: Glycoprotein required for vitamin B12 absorption in ileum
  • Gastric Emptying: Regulated release of chyme; liquids > carbs > proteins > fats

Stomach Regions

Region Location/Feature Function
Cardia Surrounds esophageal opening Entry zone, mucus secretion
Fundus Dome-shaped, superior; gas collection Reservoir, gas bubble visualization
Body Main central portion Primary digestion, mixing
Antrum Distal portion, grinding chamber Mechanical breakdown, pumping
Pylorus Distal opening with sphincter Regulates gastric emptying

Gastric Secretions (2-3 L/day)

Secretion Cell Source Function
HCl (pH 1.5-3.5) Parietal cells Activates pepsinogen, kills bacteria, denatures proteins
Pepsinogen → Pepsin Chief cells Protein digestion (begins)
Gastric Lipase Chief cells Minor fat digestion (10-30% triglycerides)
Intrinsic Factor Parietal cells B12 absorption (ileum)
Mucus/Bicarbonate Surface mucous cells Mucosal protection against acid
Gastric Motility:
• Mixing waves: 3/minute
• Gastric emptying time: Liquids (minutes) → Fats (hours)
• Regulators: Gastrin (↑ emptying), CCK/Secretin/GIP (↓ emptying)

UGI Series Positioning

Position Anatomy Visualized Clinical Use
Supine Body and antrum filled General anatomy, ulcers
Prone Fundus filled, air in antrum Fundic lesions, double contrast
Left Lateral Decubitus Air in fundus Double contrast of fundus
Upright Air-fluid levels Hiatal hernia assessment

4️⃣ Small Intestine (Digestion & Absorption) 🌀

📖 Key Definitions

  • Villi: Finger-like projections (10-40/mm²) increasing surface area
  • Microvilli (Brush Border): Apical membrane projections creating 20-fold surface area increase
  • Valvulae Conniventes (Plicae Circulares): Permanent circular folds in jejunum
  • Enterohepatic Circulation: Recycling of bile salts (95% reabsorbed in ileum)
  • Migrating Motor Complex (MMC): Housekeeper waves between meals clearing debris
  • Gastroileal Reflex: Gastric distension increases ileal emptying

Small Intestine Divisions

Segment Length/Location Key Features Primary Function
Duodenum C-shape around pancreas Brunner's glands, ampulla of Vater Chemical digestion center
Jejunum Proximal 2/5 (post-L2) Long villi, plicae circulares Primary absorption
Ileum Distal 3/5 Peyer's patches, shorter villi B12/bile salt absorption

Duodenal Anatomy

Part Description Clinical Relevance
Superior (Bulb) First part, mobile Common ulcer site
Descending Contains major duodenal papilla Ampulla of Vater location
Horizontal Crosses midline Superior mesenteric artery syndrome
Ascending Joins jejunum at flexure Duodenojejunal flexure (L2)

Digestive Enzymes & Secretions

Source Secretion Action
Pancreas Amylase Carbohydrate completion (disaccharides)
Pancreas Lipase Triglyceride hydrolysis
Pancreas Proteases (trypsin, etc.) Protein → amino acids
Brunner's Glands Bicarbonate mucus Neutralizes acidic chyme (pH 7-8)

Absorption Capacity:
• Surface area increase: 600-fold (plicae + villi + microvilli)
• Water absorption: 7-9 L/day
• Transit time: 3-5 hours

5️⃣ Large Intestine (Water Absorption) 💧

📖 Key Definitions

  • Haustra: Sacculations formed by taeniae coli contraction (incomplete folds)
  • Taeniae Coli: Three longitudinal muscle bands running length of colon
  • Epiploic Appendages: Fat-filled pouches attached to colon surface
  • Ileocecal Valve: Prevents colonic reflux; regulates ileal emptying
  • Gastrocolic Reflex: Mass movement triggered by food entering stomach
  • Anorectal Angle: 90° angle maintained by puborectalis muscle preventing incontinence

Colon Segments

6️⃣ Accessory Organs 🫁

📖 Key Definitions

  • Portal Triad: Hepatic artery, portal vein, bile duct at liver lobule corners
  • Bile Canaliculi: Small channels between hepatocytes collecting bile
  • Enterohepatic Circulation: Recycling of bile salts from ileum to liver
  • Hartmann's Pouch: Infundibulum near gallbladder neck (stone impaction site)
  • Sphincter of Oddi: Controls bile/pancreatic juice release into duodenum
  • Islets of Langerhans: Endocrine pancreas (1% of pancreatic tissue)

Liver Structure & Function

Feature Description
Weight 1.5 kg (largest gland)
Functional Unit Lobule (hepatocyte plates radiating from central vein)
Bile Production 500-1000 mL/day continuous secretion
Metabolic Functions Glycogen storage, protein synthesis, detoxification, urea synthesis

Biliary System Pathway

Bile Flow:
Hepatocytes → Bile canaliculi → Hepatic ducts (R/L) → Common hepatic duct + Cystic duct (GB) → Common bile duct + Pancreatic duct → Ampulla of Vater → Duodenum

Gallbladder Function

Function Mechanism Trigger
Concentration 10-fold water absorption Between meals
Storage 30-50 mL capacity Interdigestive period
Release Contraction CCK (fat in duodenum)

Pancreas Dual Function

Component Cell Type Secretion Function
Exocrine (99%) Acinar cells Digestive enzymes (inactive) Amylase, lipase, proteases
Exocrine Duct cells Bicarbonate-rich fluid Neutralizes chyme
Endocrine (1%) Alpha cells Glucagon Raises blood glucose
Endocrine Beta cells Insulin Lowers blood glucose

7️⃣ GI Wall Histology 🧱

📖 Key Definitions

  • Mucosa: Innermost layer (epithelium, lamina propria, muscularis mucosae)
  • Submucosa: Dense connective tissue with vessels and Meissner's plexus
  • Muscularis Externa: Inner circular and outer longitudinal muscle layers (Auerbach's plexus)
  • Serosa/Adventitia: Outermost layer (peritoneum or connective tissue)
  • Brush Border: Microvilli on intestinal absorptive cells
  • Peyer's Patches: Lymphoid tissue in ileal submucosa

GI Wall Layers

Layer Structure Epithelial Type Key Features
Mucosa Epithelium Stratified squamous (esophagus/anus) or Simple columnar (stomach/intestines) Secretion, absorption, protection
Mucosa Lamina propria Loose connective tissue Blood vessels, lymphatics, glands
Mucosa Muscularis mucosae Smooth muscle Local movement
Submucosa Dense connective tissue Areolar connective tissue Vessels, Meissner's plexus
Muscularis Externa Inner circular, outer longitudinal Smooth muscle (skeletal in upper esophagus/external anal sphincter) Peristalsis; Auerbach's plexus
Serosa/Adventitia Visceral peritoneum or CT Mesothelium (serosa) Protection, lubrication

Region-Specific Epithelial Specializations

Region Epithelial Type Specializations Function
Esophagus Non-keratinized stratified squamous Rapid turnover (7-14 days) Protection against abrasion
Stomach Simple columnar (mucous cells) Gastric pits, parietal cells, chief cells Mucus, acid, enzyme secretion
Small Intestine Simple columnar with goblet cells Villi, microvilli, crypts, Paneth cells Absorption, secretion, antimicrobial
Large Intestine Simple columnar with abundant goblet cells Straight crypts, no villi Water absorption, mucus secretion
Anal Canal Upper: Columnar; Lower: Squamous Anal columns (Morgagni) Transition zone; protection
🧠 Mnemonic: "Serosa is Superior" (outermost) → Mucosa is innermost. Layers: Mucosa, Submucosa, Muscularis, Serosa (MSMS)

8️⃣ Pathology: Short Notes 🦠

Esophageal Pathologies

Condition Pathophysiology Radiographic Findings Epithelial Changes
Achalasia Myenteric plexus degeneration → LES fails to relax Dilated esophagus, "bird's beak" tapering, air-fluid level Squamous epithelium retained; secondary inflammation
GERD/Barrett's Chronic reflux → metaplastic change Hiatal hernia, mucosal irregularity Columnar metaplasia replacing squamous (premalignant)
Carcinoma Squamous (upper) or adenocarcinoma (lower/Barrett's) Irregular stricture, mass, shouldered margins Squamous: Keratin pearls; Adeno: Glandular formation
Varices Portal hypertension → collateral dilation Serpiginous filling defects (prone best) Submucosal venous dilation; overlying epithelium intact

Gastric Pathologies

Condition Pathophysiology Radiographic Findings Epithelial Changes
Peptic Ulcer H. pylori/NSAIDs → acid-pepsin erosion Niche (crater), Hampton's line, radiating folds Mucosal break; chronic shows fibrosis
Gastric Carcinoma Intestinal-type or diffuse-type Linitis plastica, mass, ulcerated lesion Intestinal: Glandular structures; Diffuse: Signet ring cells
Lymphoma MALT or DLBCL Bulky mass, nodular folds, preserved distensibility Lymphoid infiltrate in lamina propria/submucosa
Ménétrier's TGF-α overexpression → foveolar hyperplasia Giant hypertrophied folds (proximal), hypoproteinemia Foveolar hyperplasia; decreased parietal/chief cells

Small Intestinal Pathologies

Condition Pathophysiology Radiographic Findings Epithelial Changes
Crohn's Disease Transmural chronic inflammation Skip lesions, cobblestoning, strictures, fistulas Transmural inflammation, non-caseating granulomas, fissuring ulcers
Celiac Disease Gluten sensitivity → villous atrophy Dilated bowel, flocculation, reversal of fold pattern Villous atrophy, crypt hyperplasia, intraepithelial lymphocytosis
Adenocarcinoma Often at ampulla; sporadic or FAP Annular constriction, polypoid mass, obstruction Intestinal-type: Glandular structures, nuclear atypia
Carcinoid Neuroendocrine tumor (serotonin) Submucosal mass with desmoplastic reaction Neuroendocrine cells: "Salt-and-pepper" chromatin, chromogranin+

Colonic Pathologies

Condition Pathophysiology Radiographic Findings Epithelial Changes
Adenocarcinoma Adenoma-carcinoma or serrated pathway Annular "apple-core" lesion, polypoid mass Glandular formation, nuclear pleomorphism, desmoplastic stroma
Diverticulosis Mucosa/submucosa herniation through muscularis Sac-like outpouchings (barium/air-filled) Herniated mucosa/submucosa (not full thickness)
Ulcerative Colitis Mucosal/submucosal inflammation (continuous) Loss of haustra ("lead pipe"), granular mucosa, pseudopolyps Crypt abscesses, goblet cell depletion, dysplasia risk
Ischemic Colitis Hypoperfusion → mucosal injury "Thumbprinting" (submucosal edema), segmental involvement Mucosal necrosis, hemorrhage; chronic: fibrosis, stricture

Hepatobiliary Pathologies

Condition Pathophysiology Radiographic Findings Epithelial Changes
Cholelithiasis Cholesterol supersaturation or bilirubin precipitation Radiopaque stones (10-15% on plain film); US: shadowing Cholesterolosis or chronic cholecystitis changes
Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis Autoimmune bile duct fibrosis (associated with UC) Beaded appearance (strictures/dilatations) on ERCP/MRCP Concentric "onion-skin" fibrosis; progresses to cirrhosis
Cholangiocarcinoma Bile duct malignancy (risk: PSC, liver flukes) Stricturing mass, biliary dilation, portal vein encasement Adenocarcinoma: Desmoplastic stroma, perineural invasion

Pancreatic Pathologies

Condition Pathophysiology Radiographic Findings Epithelial Changes
Acute Pancreatitis Autodigestion by activated enzymes Enlarged pancreas, peripancreatic fluid, necrosis (non-enhancing) Edematous interstitium, fat necrosis, neutrophilic infiltrates
Chronic Pancreatitis Progressive fibrosis Calcifications (pathognomonic), "chain of lakes" ductal dilation Parenchymal fibrosis, ductal distortion, intraductal calculi
Pancreatic Adenocarcinoma Ductal epithelial malignancy Hypodense mass, ductal obstruction ("double duct" sign) Dense desmoplasia, duct-like structures, perineural invasion

Contrast Media Considerations

⚠️ Critical Safety:
• Barium sulfate: Insoluble; CONTRAINDICATED if perforation suspected
• Iodinated contrast: Water-soluble; use for suspected leaks/perforations
• Always verify patient history before contrast administration

Patient Preparation

Exam Preparation
UGI/SBFT NPO 8 hours
Barium Enema Bowel prep (laxatives, enemas) essential
ERCP NPO, coagulation studies, antibiotic prophylaxis

Radiation Protection

✅ ALARA Principles:
• Fluoroscopy: Use pulsed mode, last-image-hold, collimation
• Pregnancy: Shield pelvis; consider ultrasound/MRI alternatives
• Minimize fluoroscopy time; use intermittent exposure
