1️⃣ Motor Centers Overview 🎯

📖 Key Definitions

  • Motor System: Neural structures controlling voluntary and involuntary movements via descending pathways from cortex to muscles
  • Upper Motor Neuron (UMN): Neurons from motor cortex/brainstem descending to synapse on LMNs or interneurons
  • Lower Motor Neuron (LMN): Neurons in spinal cord (ventral horn) or brainstem whose axons directly innervate skeletal muscle
  • Spastic Paralysis: Increased tone, hyperreflexia, stiffness due to UMN lesion (loss of inhibition)
  • Flaccid Paralysis: Decreased tone, atrophy, areflexia due to LMN damage

Hierarchy of Motor Control

Level Structure Function
Highest Motor Cortex, Basal Ganglia, Cerebellum Planning, initiation, coordination
Middle Brain Stem, Reticular Formation Posture, reflexes, arousal
Lowest Spinal Cord Final execution, reflexes

UMN vs LMN Comparison

Feature Upper Motor Neuron Lower Motor Neuron
Location Cortex → Brainstem/Spinal cord Spinal cord → Muscle
Function Control & modulation Final execution
Lesion Effect Spastic paralysis, hyperreflexia Flaccid paralysis, atrophy, areflexia
💡 Memory Tip: "Upper = Upbeat (spastic, hyperactive)" vs "Lower = Low/Down (flaccid, absent)"

2️⃣ Motor Neurons & Units 💪

📖 Key Definitions

  • Motor Unit: One motor neuron + ALL muscle fibers it innervates (functional unit of motor control)
  • α-motor Neuron: Large LMNs innervating extrafusal fibers for muscle contraction
  • γ-motor Neuron: Small LMNs innervating intrafusal fibers to regulate spindle sensitivity
  • Recruitment: Progressive activation of motor units to increase force
  • Asynchronous Discharge: Motor units firing at different times for smooth movement

Neuron Types

Neuron Function Target
α-motor Main muscle contraction Extrafusal fibers
γ-motor Adjust spindle sensitivity Intrafusal fibers
Interneurons Integration & modulation Other neurons
Motor Unit Size Principle:
• Small units (few fibers) = Fine control (eye muscles)
• Large units (many fibers) = Power (thigh muscles)

3️⃣ Neuronal Networks 🕸️

📖 Key Definitions

  • Divergence: One neuron branches to synapse with many targets (signal amplification)
  • Convergence: Multiple neurons synapse on one neuron (integration)
  • Temporal Facilitation: Summation of rapid successive stimuli from same source
  • Spatial Facilitation: Summation of simultaneous stimuli from different sources
  • Renshaw Inhibition: Negative feedback where motor axon collaterals excite inhibitory interneurons that inhibit the same motor neuron

Types of Neuronal Connections

Type Definition Importance
Divergence One axon → many neurons Signal amplification, safety factor
Convergence Many neurons → one neuron Integration of inputs
Occlusion Combined response < sum of individual Prevents over-activation

Inhibitory Circuits

Circuit Mechanism Example
Antagonist Inhibition Inhibits opposing muscle Biceps relaxes when triceps contracts
Renshaw Inhibition Recurrent inhibition of motor neuron Limits excessive motor output
Surround Inhibition Inhibits neighboring neurons Sharpens signal focus

4️⃣ Reflexes ⚡

📖 Key Definitions

  • Reflex: Involuntary, stereotyped response to sensory stimulus
  • Monosynaptic Reflex: Direct connection between sensory and motor neuron (one synapse)
  • Polysynaptic Reflex: Multiple synapses and interneurons involved
  • Stretch Reflex (Myotatic): Muscle contraction in response to stretch
  • Inverse Stretch Reflex: Muscle relaxation when tension is excessive (GTO-mediated)
  • Local Sign: Specific motor response pattern based on stimulus location

Classification by Synapse Number

Type Synapses Speed Examples
Monosynaptic 1 Fastest Stretch reflexes (knee jerk)
Bisynaptic 2 Fast Golgi tendon organ reflex
Polysynaptic 2+ Slower Withdrawal, cough, micturition

Clinical Stretch Reflexes

Reflex Stimulus Response Segment

Ankle Jerk Tap Achilles tendon Plantarflexion S1-S2
Biceps Jerk Tap biceps tendon Elbow flexion C5-C6
Triceps Jerk Tap triceps tendon Elbow extension C7-C8

Reflex Arc Components Comparison

5️⃣ Muscle Spindle & GTO 📏

📖 Key Definitions

  • Muscle Spindle: Sensory receptor detecting muscle length and rate of length change
  • Intrafusal Fibers: Specialized fibers inside spindle (nuclear bag and nuclear chain)
  • Nuclear Bag Fibers: Large fibers with clustered nuclei, sensitive to dynamic (rapid) stretch
  • Nuclear Chain Fibers: Smaller fibers with nuclei in row, sensitive to static (sustained) stretch
  • Annulospiral Endings: Primary sensory endings (Group Ia) wrapping central region
  • Flower-Spray Endings: Secondary endings (Group II) on nuclear chain fibers
  • Gamma Loop: Reflex circuit maintaining spindle sensitivity during contraction
  • Golgi Tendon Organ (GTO): Tension receptor at muscle-tendon junction (protective)

Spindle Structure

Component Description
Intrafusal fibers 2-10 per spindle, parallel to extrafusal
Nuclear Bag Dilated central portion, many nuclei
Nuclear Chain Thinner, shorter, linear nuclei

Spindle Innervation

Fiber Type Ending Location Function
Ia (Primary) Annulospiral Bag & Chain Length & velocity (dynamic)
II (Secondary) Flower-spray Chain only Length only (static)
γ-dynamic Motor Nuclear bag Adjusts dynamic sensitivity
γ-static Motor Nuclear chain Adjusts static sensitivity

Muscle Spindle vs GTO

Feature Muscle Spindle GTO
Location Parallel with extrafusal In series with muscle/tendon
Stimulus Muscle stretch/length Muscle tension/load
Afferent Group Ia, II Group Ib
Reflex Type Monosynaptic Bisynaptic
Response Contraction Relaxation (protective)
Key Timing Values:
• Knee jerk reaction time: 19-24 ms
• Central delay: 0.6-0.9 ms

6️⃣ Ventral Horn & Reticular Formation 🎯

📖 Key Definitions

  • Ventral Horn: Anterior spinal cord region containing LMN cell bodies
  • Final Common Pathway: The LMN - ultimate output path from CNS to muscle
  • Somatotopic Organization: Systematic arrangement of neurons by body region innervated
  • Reticular Formation: Diffuse brainstem neuron network regulating consciousness, CV control, motor functions
  • RAS (Reticular Activating System): System regulating arousal and wakefulness
  • Desynchronization: Replacement of synchronized EEG with low-voltage fast activity (arousal)

Ventral Horn Organization

Location Muscle Type Example
Medial Trunk muscles Postural muscles
Lateral Extremities (distal) Hand, foot muscles
Dorsal Flexor muscles Biceps, hamstrings
Ventral Extensor muscles Triceps, quadriceps
🎯 Key Concept: Ventral horn cells = "Final Common Pathway" (last output from CNS to muscle)

Reticular Formation Functions

Center Function
Cardiovascular Blood pressure control
Respiratory Breathing regulation
Cardiac Heart rate control
RAS Consciousness/wakefulness

7️⃣ EEG & Sleep 😴

📖 Key Definitions

  • EEG: Recording of brain electrical activity via scalp electrodes
  • Alpha Waves: 8-13 Hz, relaxed wakefulness, eyes closed
  • Beta Waves: 13-30 Hz, alert wakefulness, active thinking
  • Theta Waves: 4-7 Hz, drowsiness and light sleep
  • Delta Waves: 0.5-4 Hz, deep sleep
  • Sleep Spindles: Brief 10-14 Hz bursts in Stage 2 sleep
  • K-Complexes: High-amplitude biphasic waves in Stage 2
  • PGO Spikes: Ponto-Geniculo-Occipital spikes characteristic of REM
  • REM Sleep: Rapid eye movement sleep with muscle atonia and dreaming

EEG Wave Types

Wave Frequency State Location
Alpha (α) 8-13 Hz Rest, eyes closed Frontal, Occipital
Beta (β) 13-30 Hz Alert, active thinking Frontal
Theta (θ) 4-7 Hz Drowsiness, sleep Hippocampus
Delta (δ) 0.5-4 Hz Deep sleep Diffuse

Sleep Stages (Non-REM)

Stage EEG Pattern Characteristics
Stage 1 Theta waves (4-6 Hz) Drowsy, light sleep
Stage 2 Sleep spindles + K-complexes HR and temperature fall
Stage 3 Delta waves begin Slow-wave sleep begins
Stage 4 Delta waves (1-2 Hz) Deep sleep, maximum slowing

REM vs Non-REM Sleep

Feature REM Sleep Non-REM Sleep
EEG Desynchronized (awake-like) Synchronized slow waves
Eye Movement Rapid None
Muscle Tone Hypotonia (paralysis) Variable, higher
Dreams Vivid, frequent Rare, less vivid
Function Memory consolidation Physical restoration
🧠 Mnemonic: "BATS D" (frequency high to low): Beta, Alpha, Theta, Sleep spindles, Delta

8️⃣ Cerebellum 🧠

📖 Key Definitions

  • Cerebellum: "Little brain" posterior to brainstem, coordinates precision and timing
  • Archicerebellum: Oldest part (flocculonodular), vestibular connections, balance
  • Spinocerebellum: Intermediate portion, proprioceptive input, ongoing movement coordination
  • Neocerebellum: Newest/largest (lateral hemispheres), movement planning
  • Purkinje Cells: Sole output neurons of cerebellar cortex (inhibitory)
  • Ataxia: Loss of coordination, unsteady gait
  • Dysmetria: Inability to judge distance (overshooting/undershooting)
  • Adiadochokinesia: Inability to perform rapid alternating movements
  • Intention Tremor: Tremor increasing during voluntary movement toward target

Functional Divisions

Division Other Names Input Function
Archicerebellum Vestibulocerebellum Vestibular system Balance, equilibrium
Spinocerebellum Paleocerebellum Proprioception, cortex Coordinate ongoing movements
Neocerebellum Cerebrocerebellum Motor cortex, pons Planning, programming

9️⃣ Basal Ganglia ⚙️

📖 Key Definitions

  • Basal Ganglia: Subcortical nuclei at forebrain base (caudate, putamen, globus pallidus, substantia nigra, STN)
  • Corpus Striatum: Caudate + putamen (main input zone)
  • Direct Pathway: Cortex → striatum → GPi/SNr → thalamus → cortex (facilitates movement)
  • Indirect Pathway: Cortex → striatum → GPe → STN → GPi/SNr → thalamus → cortex (inhibits movement)
  • Nigrostriatal Pathway: Dopaminergic projection from SNc to striatum
  • Parkinson's Disease: Degeneration of nigrostriatal dopaminergic neurons → TRAP symptoms
  • Huntington's Disease (Chorea): Striatal degeneration → dance-like movements
  • Hemiballismus: Violent flinging movements from contralateral STN lesion

Basal Ganglia Components

Structure Subdivisions Key Connections
Caudate nucleus Head, body, tail Receives cortical input
Putamen - With caudate = corpus striatum
Globus pallidus External (GPe), Internal (GPi) Main output to thalamus
Substantia nigra Pars compacta (SNc), Pars reticularis (SNr) Dopaminergic nigrostriatal
Subthalamus - Excitatory with GP

Parkinson's Disease (TRAP)

Symptom Description
Tremor at rest Pill-rolling tremor, decreases with movement

Postural Instability Late feature, balance problems

Ballismus Subthalamus Violent flinging movements
Athetosis Corpus striatum Slow, writhing movements
🧠 Mnemonic: "Dope Moves You" - Dopamine (from SNc) is essential for movement

🔟 Hypothalamus 🌡️

📖 Key Definitions

  • Hypothalamus: Small diencephalic region regulating homeostasis, autonomic functions, endocrine
  • Feeding Center: Lateral hypothalamic area stimulating hunger
  • Satiety Center: Ventromedial nucleus inhibiting feeding
  • ADH (Vasopressin): Synthesized in hypothalamus, released from posterior pituitary for water retention
  • Oxytocin: Uterine contraction and milk ejection hormone
  • Circadian Rhythm: ~24-hour biological cycle regulated by suprachiasmatic nucleus
  • Poikilothermia: Inability to maintain constant body temperature

Hypothalamic Regions & Functions

Region Nuclei Main Functions
Anterior Paraventricular, Supraoptic, Preoptic Heat loss, ADH/oxytocin, parasympathetic
Posterior Posterior, Dorsomedial, Ventromedial Heat conservation, sympathetic, feeding
Lateral Diffuse nuclei Hunger, thirst, emotions, reward

Hypothalamic Functions Summary

Function Center Stimulation Effect Destruction Effect
Feeding Lateral hypothalamus Hunger, eating Lethal starvation
Satiety Ventromedial nucleus Fullness Voracious eating, obesity
Thirst Lateral hypothalamus Drinking Adipsia (no drinking)
Water conservation Supraoptic (ADH) Water reabsorption Diabetes insipidus
Temperature Anterior (cool) & Posterior (heat) Sweating/shivering Poikilothermia
🧠 Mnemonic: "HOT BED" - Hunger, Osmoregulation, Temperature, Blood pressure, Endocrine, Drives (sex, thirst)

1️⃣1️⃣ Limbic System ❤️

📖 Key Definitions

  • Limbic System: Brain structures on both sides of thalamus involved in emotion, motivation, memory
  • Hippocampus: Seahorse-shaped medial temporal structure crucial for memory formation
  • Amygdala: Almond-shaped nucleus for emotional processing (fear, aggression)
  • Fornix: C-shaped tract connecting hippocampus to mammillary bodies
  • Papez Circuit: Neural circuit proposed as anatomical substrate of emotion
  • Kluver-Bucy Syndrome: Bilateral amygdala damage → placidity, hyperorality, hypersexuality
  • Anterograde Amnesia: Inability to form new memories (bilateral hippocampal lesion)

Mammillary → Anterior thalamus Mammillothalamic tract Relay to cortex
Anterior thalamus → Cingulate Thalamocortical fibers Cortical processing
Cingulate → Hippocampus Cingulum Completes circuit

1️⃣2️⃣ Motor Tracts 🛤️

📖 Key Definitions

  • Pyramidal Tract: Main voluntary motor pathway from cortex through medullary pyramids
  • Extrapyramidal Tract: Motor pathways excluding direct corticospinal (basal ganglia, brainstem nuclei)
  • Corticospinal Tract: Pathway from motor cortex to spinal cord (lateral and anterior)
  • Pyramidal Decussation: Crossing of 80% of corticospinal fibers in caudal medulla
  • Internal Capsule: White matter structure containing concentrated fiber tracts
  • Reticulospinal Tract: From reticular formation to spinal cord (posture, automatic movements)
  • Vestibulospinal Tract: From vestibular nuclei (postural responses to gravity)

Pyramidal vs Extrapyramidal

Feature Pyramidal Extrapyramidal
Anatomy Direct cortex to spinal cord Indirect, multiple synapses
Passes through Medullary pyramids Does NOT pass through pyramids
Function Fine, skilled voluntary movement Posture, tone, basic movements
Control Distal limb muscles Proximal, axial muscles
Lesion Spastic paralysis, loss of fine control Various movement disorders

Corticospinal Tract Details

Feature Description
Origin Motor cortex
Decussation 80% at medulla (pyramidal decussation)
Lateral tract 80%, crosses, distal muscles
Anterior tract 20%, ipsilateral, axial muscles

Extrapyramidal Tracts

Tract Origin Function
Medial reticulospinal Pontine reticular formation Posture, startle reflex
Lateral reticulospinal Medullary reticular formation Inhibitory, reflex modulation
Vestibulospinal Vestibular nuclei Posture, anti-gravity, balance
Rubrospinal Red nucleus Flexor muscle control
Tectospinal Superior colliculus Head turning, visual reflexes

• "Pyramids are for Precision" (Pyramidal = fine, skilled)
• "Extra is for Everything else" (Extrapyramidal = posture, tone)
